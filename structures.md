# Structural patterns

The tells that survive vocabulary cleanup. These are why text still "reads AI"
even when no banned word appears.

## Frequency-capped (fine once, slop when repeated)

- **The correlative pivot: "Not X. It is Y." / "X isn't about A, it's about B."**
  The single most recognizable LLM cadence in 2026. One per piece is a rhetorical
  move. Three or more is a fingerprint. Cap at one.
- **Rule of three.** "Fast, cheap, and reliable." Fine occasionally. When every
  list is a triad and every sentence has three clauses, it is mechanical. Vary
  list length; sometimes two items, sometimes four.
- **Dramatic one-to-three-word fragments.** "Full stop." "Every time." "That's
  it." Fine sparingly for emphasis. A paragraph built from them is the
  over-corrected slop, not the fix.
- **Sentence-initial "And" / "But".** Natural in moderation. Match a real human
  baseline, not one per sentence.
- **Mirror-symmetry aphorisms.** "They look like conviction and read like
  confusion." Perfect chiasmus is cheap for a model and rare for a human. At most
  one per piece.

## Cut on sight

- **Rhetorical-question scaffolding.** "What if I told you...?" "Here's the
  question:" "Sound familiar?" State the claim directly.
- **The setup-payoff tease.** "The result? Remarkable." "The reason is simple."
  Just give the result.
- **Anaphora with generic personas.** "Why does this clinic still use paper? Why
  does this shop still run on WhatsApp?" with invented, nameless examples. If the
  example is real, name it. If not, cut it.
- **Restate-everything endings.** A closing paragraph that summarizes each earlier
  point. End on the strongest single idea instead.
- **The permission-granting close.** "And that's okay." "And that's perfectly
  normal." Delete.

## Preserve (these are NOT slop)

- The author's real, recurring quirks when a voice profile marks them protected.
- Genuine parallelism that carries meaning.
- Lists when the content is genuinely a list (steps, options, specs).
