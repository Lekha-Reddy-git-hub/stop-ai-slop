# What NOT to flag (do not over-correct)

This is the file that keeps deslop from becoming the second slop. A clean human
writer hits many "AI patterns" without any AI involvement. Before you cut, check
that you are not gutting real prose. The tool's job is to remove the machine's
fingerprint, not to sand every writer down to the same neutral paste.

Rule: look for CLUSTERS of tells, not isolated ones. A single em dash means
nothing. Em dashes plus rule-of-three plus "vibrant tapestry" plus a "Conclusion"
section is a confession. One "however" is not.

## Not reliable indicators on their own

- **Polish.** Perfect grammar and consistent style. Many writers are professionals
  or have been edited. Clean does not mean AI.
- **Mixed casual and formal registers.** Often a person in a technical field, a
  young writer, or a neurodivergent prose habit, not a chatbot.
- **"Bland" or "robotic" prose.** AI has specific tells. Generic dryness without
  those tells is just dry writing.
- **Formal or academic vocabulary.** AI overuses specific fancy words, not all fancy
  words. Do not flatten "ostensibly" or "constituent" for sounding brainy.
- **A single em dash, or curly quotes alone.** Editors, journalists, and every
  default word processor produce these. Evidence only when stacked with other tells.
- **One common transition word.** "Additionally," "moreover," "consequently" are AI
  coded only when piled up.
- **One short emphatic sentence.** Humans use clipped sentences to land a point.
  Flag staccato only when several fragments run together and inflate the tone.
- **"Honestly" or "look" mid-sentence.** Ordinary in casual writing. The tell is the
  standalone theatrical opener, not the word.
- **Unsourced claims.** Most of the web is unsourced. It proves nothing on its own.

## Signs of human writing (protect these)

When you see these, lean toward leaving the prose alone. Over-editing destroys what
makes a piece sound like a person.

- **Specific, hard-to-fabricate detail.** A real address, a weird quote, "the lawyer
  who used to work upstairs from my dentist." LLMs round specifics off; humans hoard
  them.
- **Mixed feelings and unresolved tension.** "I think this is mostly good, but it
  bothers me and I cannot say why." LLMs default to clean takes.
- **Dated, era-bound references.** Slang, memes, in-jokes tied to a year and a
  subculture.
- **Editorial choices the writer can defend.** If they can explain why they cut a
  word, that is a human signal.
- **Real variety in sentence length.** Human writing alternates short and long. Do
  not flatten it to an even cadence, and do not chop it into all-short slop either.
- **Genuine asides and self-corrections.** "(I keep wanting to say almost here, but
  it really was certain.)"
- **The author's protected pet phrases.** If a voice profile marks a habit as this
  person's own, it stays, even when it resembles a listed tell.

## The over-polish flag

If your rewrite is markedly cleaner, more uniform, and more perfect than the
author's own baseline, you have gone too far. Real voices are uneven. Do not
fabricate imperfection to fake it; just stop over-editing what the author wrote.
