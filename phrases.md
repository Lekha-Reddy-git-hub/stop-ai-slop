# Phrases, tiered

Not a blanket banlist. Each tier is handled differently. A phrase in "cut unless
context" is fine when the sentence genuinely needs it.

## Tier 1: Always cut (vocabulary tells)

These words spiked in AI text after 2023 (Kobak et al. 2025 measured "delves"
rising 28-fold). They rarely earn their place. Replace with a plain word.

delve, tapestry, testament (to), realm, boasts, nestled, bustling, underscore(s),
elevate, robust, seamless, myriad, plethora, leverage (as verb), utilize,
navigate (challenges), unpack (an idea), landscape (as metaphor), ecosystem
(as metaphor), game-changer, deep dive, at the end of the day, in today's
fast-paced world, in the ever-evolving.

## Tier 1: Always cut (dead phrases, no meaning)

These add zero information. Delete, do not replace.

"It's worth noting that", "It's important to note", "Needless to say",
"When it comes to", "At its core", "In essence", "Let that sink in",
"Make no mistake", "Here's the thing", "The truth is", "Let me be clear",
"That said" (as reflexive filler), "Full stop." / "Period." (as emphasis).

## Tier 2: Cut unless context justifies it

Sometimes exactly right. Keep when the meaning depends on it; cut when reflexive.

| Phrase | Keep when | Cut when |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| "It turns out" | reporting a genuine surprise finding | throat-clearing before a plain fact |
| "Interestingly," | the reader could not predict it | decorating an ordinary sentence |
| "Importantly," | ranking against other points | every third sentence |
| "Ultimately," | a real conclusion after weighing | restating what you just said |
| "That is to say" | a genuine restatement clarifies | padding |

## Tier 2: Business jargon (replace with plain language)

Navigate → handle, address. Lean into → accept, use. Circle back → return to.
Moving forward → next, from now. On the same page → agreed. Double down →
commit, increase. Take a step back → reconsider.

## Tier 3: Fake specificity (cut or make real)

"Studies show", "Research suggests", "Experts agree", "It is widely known",
"Many believe" with no named source. Either name the source or cut the claim.

## Email and letter openers (always cut)

"I hope this email finds you well", "I hope you're doing well", "I wanted to
reach out", "I am writing to inform you", "I am writing this letter to".
